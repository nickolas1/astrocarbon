where do these numbers come from?
Each region was assigned a representative airport, chosen to be near either an obvious concentration of astronomers or the geographical center of the region. These are: Africa: JNB; Antarctica: 'Antarctica'; Asia: PEK; Europe: AMS; Hawaii: HNL; Northern Americas: ORD; Oceania: SYD; South America: SCL. The CO2 cost of travel between each airport was calculated at travelnav.com, which seems broadly similar to other estimators and would allow me to type in Antarctica as a destination. Air travel within regions was assigned a CO2 cost of 0.5 tons; this may overestimate some trips and underestimate others. Exceptions are Hawaii and Antarctica, where inter-island and snowy flights count for 0.1 tons.
Personal computing costs were calculated assuming 60W during normal use, which is in the right ballpark. This is a pretty minor contributor. HPC costs were calculated assuming 10 GFLOPS per core, and 750 MFLOPS/W as representative of a middle-of-the pack member of the Green 500. The Green 500 list spans an order of magnitude in efficiency, so this is a particularly error-prone number. Yearly Watt-hours were turned into CO2 costs with the conversion 0.5 kg CO2 / kWh; this is probably good to a factor of a couple unless you live in a country with a particularly high fraction of nuclear or hydro power.
The data for the world's per-capita CO2 usage comes from CDIAC, and are from 2010. These numbers include fossil-fuel burning, cement production, and gas flaring.
isn't this way too simplistic?
Probably. $\sim$ is implied throughout. I'd bet that these assumptions would pass astronomical peer review.
The precise number isn't so important. The point is that if you are a typical astronomer, your career costs the climate about as much as an additional person.
why did you do this?
I think climate change is the greatest single moral challenge we face (not to put too fine a point on it). Future generations, including the youngest current one, will treat all the other crises and conflicts of our lives as footnotes to our response to climate change. I think we should spare some thought to how our activities impact the world while we study space.
this feels like you're picking on astronomers. why pick on astronomers?
I am currently an astronomer.
why did you divide the world up so terribly?
the world is large, and I wanted to keep the options manageable. I chose to follow FIFA's confederations, with a few exceptions (the CONCACAF members in South America; Greenland; Hawaii). I sincerely don't want to cause any distress by the way I regionalized the world, so please let me know if I made a bad choice.
